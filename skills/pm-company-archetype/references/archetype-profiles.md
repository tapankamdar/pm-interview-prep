# Archetype Profiles

Detailed interview intelligence by company type.

---

## FAANG / Big Tech

**Interview structure:** Structured multi-round loops. Behavioral and product sense rounds are typically separate. Bar raiser or equivalent adds an independent calibration round. Each interviewer owns a specific evaluation dimension.

**What they optimize for:** Calibrated seniority match. A Director who answers like a Senior PM will be leveled down or passed. The bar isn't just "good PM" — it's "good PM at this level at this company's scale."

**Scoring emphasis:** Substance and credibility at scale. "I led a team of 200" needs to show what leading 200 actually required differently from leading 20. Scale must be functional, not decorative.

**Common mistakes:** 
- Answers that would work at any company (need Google/Meta/Amazon-specific insight)
- Under-calibrating on organizational complexity (the cross-functional alignment is the hard part, not the product decision)
- Missing the bar raiser — this person is evaluating caliber vs. company, not just fit for the role

---

## Hypergrowth SaaS

**Interview structure:** Often faster and less structured than FAANG. Founder or C-suite often involved early. Timeline moves quickly. Culture is accessible and direct.

**What they optimize for:** Ownership and speed. "I decided X, here's what I owned, here's the number it moved." They are not looking for polished PM process — they're looking for someone who acts like an executive, gets measured like one.

**Scoring emphasis:** Business outcome and decision ownership. The story that scores highest is: "We had a constraint, I made a call, I owned the outcome, here's what happened." The worst answer is a process story with no decision and no result.

**Common mistakes:**
- Over-polishing (signals big-company PM who needs process)
- Waiting to be told the vision (they want you to bring one)
- Not tying stories to business metrics

---

## Enterprise / B2B

**Interview structure:** Often involves multiple stakeholder conversations rather than a formal loop. Change management and org navigation are evaluated heavily.

**What they optimize for:** Ability to drive change in a complex organization with many stakeholders, long sales cycles, and conservative buyers. They want someone who can get a large organization to adopt something new.

**Scoring emphasis:** Stakeholder navigation, change management, and executive communication. A product decision that ignores the political/organizational dimension is incomplete.

**Common mistakes:**
- Answers that assume you can move fast (enterprise moves slower, and the PM has to know why that's rational, not just frustrating)
- Undervaluing relationships and trust-building as a product skill
- Treating "we shipped it" as the outcome — in enterprise, adoption is the outcome

---

## AI-first / Frontier

**What they optimize for:** AI product conviction. Not "have you used LLMs?" but "do you have a defensible POV on where AI creates durable value vs. where it's a feature?" They want someone who has actually thought hard about this, not someone who has read the same think pieces.

**Scoring emphasis:** Differentiation and credibility on AI-specific challenges: evaluation, trust, grounding, hallucination risk, user behavior with AI systems. Someone who has shipped AI and knows what broke is more valuable than someone who has opinions about what to build.

**The question behind the question:** "Do you understand what makes AI products different from traditional software?" Not reliability curves — trust dynamics. Not model selection — evaluation infrastructure.

---

## Marketplace / Platform

**What they optimize for:** Supply-demand balance and multi-sided platform thinking. The candidate who sees a marketplace as a two-sided (or three-sided) system scores higher than the one who only thinks about the rider/buyer/consumer side.

**Scoring emphasis:** Economics (unit economics, LTV/CAC, contribution margin), network effects (how supply and demand reinforce each other), and the correct identification of which side of the marketplace is the constraint at different growth stages.

**Common mistakes:**
- One-sided analysis (focusing only on riders, not drivers; only on consumers, not retailers)
- Missing the incentive alignment problem (what makes a driver or seller choose to stay vs. leave)
- Treating "grow both sides" as a strategy rather than the starting question

---

## Travel / Commerce

**What they optimize for:** Transaction optimization and search/discovery at scale. Personalization that moves conversion. An understanding of the full customer journey from intent to booking to post-trip.

**Scoring emphasis:** Metrics fluency (conversion rate, AOV, repeat booking rate, NPS) and user intent modeling. The ability to identify what the user actually wants vs. what they searched for.
