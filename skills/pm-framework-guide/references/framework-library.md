# Framework Library

Decision trees and usage guidance for common PM interview frameworks.

---

## CIRCLES (Product Design)

**Use when:** IC or APM level, product sense questions where the structure itself signals thoroughness.

**Avoid when:** Director+ level, or when the company has a known product philosophy that the framework ignores.

**Adaptation for senior roles:** Skip the acronym. Build the same logic naturally: "First I want to understand who we're building for and what they're struggling with — then I'll work toward solutions." Same steps, no template signal.

**The liability:** CIRCLES treats all steps as equal. In practice, user segmentation and problem prioritization are 70% of the score. Don't spend equal time on each step.

---

## HEART (Google's UX Metrics)

**Use when:** Measuring user experience quality for consumer products — Happiness, Engagement, Adoption, Retention, Task success.

**Avoid when:** B2B, marketplace, or AI products where these dimensions don't map cleanly. Don't force HEART onto a supply-demand balance problem.

**The right signal:** "I think about user experience measurement across a few dimensions — " and then describe the ones that fit, without naming the framework.

---

## AARRR / Pirate Metrics (Growth)

**Use when:** Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral — product-led growth contexts, consumer apps, early-stage company discussion.

**Avoid when:** Enterprise SaaS (sales-led), marketplace (wrong model), or when the question is about a specific metric, not the full funnel.

**Senior adaptation:** Know which stage of AARRR is the actual bottleneck for this company, and lead there. Don't walk through all five if the problem is obviously a retention question.

---

## RICE / ICE (Prioritization)

**Use when:** Structuring a prioritization decision, showing you have a framework for comparing unlike things.

**Avoid when:** The question is actually about strategic tradeoffs, not feature ranking. RICE applied to a "what would you work on in year 1?" question signals tactical thinking.

**The insight layer:** RICE gives you a number. The PM interview question is why you assigned those scores — that's where differentiation lives. Always explain the reasoning behind the scores, not just the output.

---

## North Star Metric

**Not a framework — a thinking discipline.** The north star should be the metric that best captures whether customers are getting real value, is leadable (the team can influence it), and predicts long-term business health.

**Common mistakes:**
- Choosing a vanity metric (DAU when engagement quality is what matters)
- Choosing a metric the team can't influence
- Choosing a metric that can be gamed (session count, not session quality)

**The test:** "If this metric goes up but customers aren't better off, is that a win?" If yes, it's the wrong metric.

## Jobs to Be Done (JTBD)

**Use when:** Deep product sense questions, user research discussion, why a product succeeds or fails.

**The frame:** "People don't buy products — they hire them to do a job. What job is the user hiring this product for?" Forces specificity about actual user motivation vs. demographic description.

**Senior application:** Use JTBD language naturally without naming it. "The core job this product is hired for is..." signals earned insight, not framework knowledge.

---

## The 3-Pillar Strategy Structure

**Use when:** "How would you approach this role?", "What's your vision for X?", "What would you focus on in year 1?"

**Rules:**
1. Each pillar must be grounded in a specific company fact — not a generic priority
2. The pillars must form a system, not three independent bets (explain the sequencing logic)
3. Include the 1-line version for when interrupted: "Three things that have to work together as a system..."
4. Adapt to role type: product-building roles get product pillars; operational roles get function pillars; GM roles get P&L pillars

**Credibility audit:** If a pillar could be reused at a different company with a find-replace, it fails.
