# Origin Story Guide

## The Structure

A strong origin story has three beats:

**Beat 1 — The problem thread (15–20 seconds)**
Name the problem the candidate has been working on across their career. Not a job title, not a company — a problem. The more specific, the better.

*Example:* "I've spent the last several years working on one problem: how do you give AI systems enough context about what a user actually needs to produce useful output, not just plausible output."

**Beat 2 — The career evidence (20–25 seconds)**
Two or three examples of where they've worked on that problem, each from a different angle. Short, specific, evidence-tagged.

*Example:* "At one company, at the on-device layer — had to build a live model of user context without sending anything to the cloud. At another, at platform scale — how does a product with 200M daily users surface the right intelligence at the right moment."

**Beat 3 — Why this company, why now (15–20 seconds)**
Connect the thread to this specific company's version of the problem. Name what's unique about them (not just their mission). Name the timing if there is one.

*Example:* "Both are fundamentally context, grounding, and user intent problems — which is exactly what this role is. And the team is being formed now, which means there's real shaping work to do. That's the combination I'm looking for."

## The Quality Spectrum

### Strong (target)

The career arc leads *inevitably* to this company's specific product problem. "I've been working on [exact problem] from [angle 1] and [angle 2] for five years. This company is the first place where that's the whole job."

**Example — enterprise AI company (legal/workflow domain):**

> "Every significant business decision eventually becomes a contract. And almost every time, that contract is where momentum goes to slow down — where the work that happened in meetings, in relationships, in strategy sessions, gets handed off to a process that feels like it belongs to a different decade.
>
> Across my product leadership journey, I've focused on one problem: how to embed intelligence into products so people can stay focused on what matters, not manage the system around it.
>
> The opportunity here is to build AI that a domain expert will stake their reputation on. Not just more capable — but trustworthy. That's a different problem, and a harder one. It's also the one I've been working toward."

*Why it works:* Opens with a specific business observation unique to this domain. Career thread is clear. The closing raises the specific bar this company faces — trustworthy AI for high-stakes decisions.

---

**Example — enterprise search / organizational intelligence platform:**

> "I've spent the last several years working on a problem that turns out to be central to what this role is about — how do you give AI systems enough context about what a user actually needs to produce useful output, not just plausible output.
>
> At one company, on-device — had to build a live model of user context from local signals only, without sending data to the cloud. At another, at platform scale — how does a product with hundreds of millions of daily active users surface the right intelligence at the right moment.
>
> Both are fundamentally context, grounding, and user intent problems — which is exactly what this role addresses. And the team is being formed now, which means there's real shaping work to do. That's the combination I'm looking for."

*Why it works:* Specific problem named. Two angles shown. Company-specific tie — names the actual product area and the timing signal.

---

### Solid

Personal connection to the mission with a clean bridge to the role. Authentic and vivid. Loose on the specific product problem but the mission fit is real.

**Example — travel platform:**

> "I grew up in a place where travel was deeply meaningful — it opened perspectives, created connections, and brought me closer to people.
>
> Across my career, I've led teams to build and scale AI-driven platforms — ambient assistants, generative agents, intent-based search engines.
>
> This company's mission to power travel for everyone resonates because it's ultimately about access, connection, and growth. This role is where AI platform work maps directly to that mission."

*What could be stronger:* Add a specific bridge — what is the company's specific AI product challenge that this candidate's background uniquely positions them to solve?

---

### Weak (example of what to fix)

Personal connection to the broader theme without connecting to the specific product challenge. Could apply to multiple companies in the same space.

**Example — grocery / food delivery platform:**

> "I believe tech should meet communities where their needs are. I've volunteered with food banks helping get food to families who need it. A close friend relied on this platform during a difficult time in her life."

*Why it's weak:* Authentic and human, but the bridge to this company's specific product challenge is absent. The same story works for any food, grocery, or community company.

*Fix direction:* "What makes this company's challenge specific is [the B2B enterprise platform problem / AI personalization for retailers and brands]. My experience building AI at platform scale and SMB-facing AI is the exact combination that problem requires. That's what drew me here."

## Credibility Gradient Summary

| Level | Description | Pass/Fail |
|---|---|---|
| Mission alignment | "I believe in your mission" | Fails — anyone can say this |
| Industry insight | "I think [trend] creates opportunity here" | Borderline |
| Company-specific problem | "The specific problem you face is [X], visible in [signal]" | Passes |
| Career arc connection | "Your problem is the same one I've been working on from [angle 1] and [angle 2]" | Strong pass |
| Earned insight | "What I learned from [experience] that applies here: [counterintuitive insight]" | Strongest |
