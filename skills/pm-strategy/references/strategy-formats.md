# Strategy Formats by Role Type

## Product-Building Role (IC to Lead PM)

Pillars describe what you'd build, in what order, and why that sequence.

**Template:**
- Pillar 1: The foundational bet — the thing that has to work before anything else
- Pillar 2: The flagship product built on that foundation
- Pillar 3: The mechanism that makes the first two trustworthy/scalable/defensible

**Example — enterprise AI platform (legal/workflow domain):**
- Pillar 1: Shared agentic infrastructure — extract common patterns from individual agents into scaffolding; new agents in weeks, not months
- Pillar 2: Flagship AI product — standards-first onboarding closes the adoption gap; the AI starts from the customer's bar, not a generic one
- Pillar 3: Trust engine — expert-annotated golden datasets, continuous regression testing, customer-visible accuracy scores

Soundbite: *"Infrastructure first, the flagship second, the trust engine third — these aren't three workstreams, they're a system."*

## Platform / Org Leader (Director, VP)

Pillars describe the strategic architecture — the shift you're enabling, not the features you'd ship.

**Template:**
- Pillar 1: The context model / foundational layer (Year 1)
- Pillar 2: The primary product surface or agent interface (Year 2)
- Pillar 3: The organizational or ecosystem layer (Year 3+)

**Example — enterprise search / organizational intelligence:**
- Context over query (live model of user state, not keyword matching)
- Authority over relevance (trust as a ranking signal, not just keyword match)
- Action over retrieval (search ends with doing something, not finding something)

3-year vision variant adds: current state → Year 1 → Year 2 → Year 3 → end state, each with a "from / to" framing.

Soundbite: *"From finding things to knowing things — that's the arc."*

## Functional / Operational Leader (Support, Growth, Ops)

Pillars describe how you'd run the function — the operational thesis, not a product roadmap.

**Template:**
- Pillar 1: Eliminate the root cause (reduce inbound / avoidable work)
- Pillar 2: Automate resolution (scale throughput without scaling headcount)
- Pillar 3: Elevate the human layer (make frontline the highest-value work)

**Example — AI-powered customer support function:**
- Fewer avoidable contacts ("Use support as a radar, not a mop — mine tickets to drive product improvements")
- Automate fast, fair resolutions ("Automation is the lever; lower cost-to-serve and fairer outcomes are the goal")
- Make the frontline the hero ("Agents become experts, not ticket handlers")

Soundbite: *"Make support the lowest-cost, highest-trust learning engine."*

**Key distinction from product-building format:** Pillars are measured in operational metrics (cost-to-serve, handle time, CSAT, deflection rate), not product metrics (DAU, conversion, retention).

---

## GM / Business Owner

Pillars describe the growth engine — the P&L levers and expansion logic.

**Template:**
- Pillar 1: Protect and expand the core (retention, monetization of existing base)
- Pillar 2: Unlock the adjacent growth opportunity (new segment, new surface, new motion)
- Pillar 3: Build the compounding advantage (data, network effect, ecosystem lock-in)

**Soundbite pattern:** *"[Business outcome] through [mechanism] — that's the engine."*

## The 3-Year Vision (HM Rounds Only)

For HM conversations, the 3 pillars often need a companion piece: the layered vision showing where this role leads in 3 years. Use a table:

| Stage | Interface | Primary user | What's possible |
|---|---|---|---|
| Today | [Current state] | [Current user] | [Current capability] |
| Year 1 | [First shift] | [Same/expanded] | [New capability] |
| Year 2 | [Second shift] | [Expanded] | [Bigger capability] |
| Year 3 | [End state] | [Full vision] | [Full potential] |

The 1-line if interrupted: *"From [X] to [Y] — that's the arc."*
